CSSI Mindsets

As an instructor, it’s your job to not only say these to students on day 1, but also to reinforce these norms through everything you do

  • Failure is a normal, healthy, and necessary part of learning to code.
  • Risk-taking is critically important.
  • Support each other.
  • No code snobs.
  • The point is learning / growth; the code is incidental.
  • You’re going to build something cool.

The Structure of a Day

Each day is divided into three major components, which correspond to the “I do, we do, you do” instructional model of gradual release:

  • Supplemental Resources (I do) - Supplemental material will be sent out which may be a video to watch, an article or textbook excerpt to read, or some other method of engaging with the day’s content more passively. It is not wise to expect students to have mastered this content before the code-along.
  • Code-along (we do) - This is the bulk of the content written here in this curriculum. These code-alongs are opportunities for you to demonstrate how to build up to a target, and to model the debugging and thought processes that your students will need to use as they move forward.
  • Independent Practice Extensions - after each lesson, students should take the finished code and add to it. Ideas for how the independent practice can be framed, as well as specific stretch activities for each day are linked in the lessons.

Lesson Prep

Each lesson is written in the order that you might share it with students, showing how to go from starter code to a finished product. In order to master the content for any given lesson, however, it is recommended that you process it in a slightly different way.

  1. Start by looking at the finished code. Demo the finished product, and try to infer what new things students will be grappling with that day that they have not seen before.
  2. Then go back to the starter code, and follow the stepped solution to see how we will demo this for students.
  3. Flag any concepts that you think your students might need additional reinforcement with to your teaching team, and script out what you might way to say, or what questions you might want to ask in order to get students to a working understanding of that new concept. For example, if the previous day was our first-ever introduction to functions, and students in your cohort struggled, you may want to make extra time to talk through how the functions are used today.
  4. Practice teaching this, speaking aloud whatever you plan to say to students.
  5. When teaching, if you have two monitors, is recommended that you have the stepped solution open in one window while you work, so that you don’t accidentally skip something which would lead to a nonproductive bug. Some bugs are great, but the stepped solution is organized to make sure that each new item leads naturally to the curiosity that might inspire the next (unless otherwise indicated). If you don’t have two monitors, consider printing out, transcribing, or pulling the stepped solution up on your phone.

Presentation Guide

There are many different ways to lead a remote code-along excellently. Here are some of the through lines:

Make sure students CAN follow along.

Use some combination of the following to help ensure that students have the capacity to follow along as you go.

Layout:

  • Prefer to share you whole screen whenever possible. Things like pop-up messages from alert() are usually not visible to students if you are only sharing a tab or a window.
  • Make sure the font size on your screen is readable on smaller screens as you’re screen sharing.
  • Make sure that when you ask students to code along, that you give them ample time to get their environments up and running before writing your first line of code.
  • If you’re going to expect them to follow along in a split-screen desktop (half yours, half theirs), make sure to tell them that, and then give them time to set up before moving forward. If you do this, make sure to have your window half-screen as well - you want the aspect ratio of your shared tab to be reasonably similar to the window they’re viewing your code in (otherwise it will be shrunk down to fit and become impossible to read).

Pacing:

  • Whenever you have a visible indicator of a certain bar of success, pause and let all students confirm that they’ve caught up.
  • If you’re going to ask students to switch between “listening” and “coding” modes, plan to allow extra time for them to transition back and forth between windows, and have TAs signal to you when you’re moving too fast.

Expert moves:

  • Have some students share their edit links to a Glitch project with their TAs. The TAs can then follow along live with those students and let you know when you’re moving faster than the student. Each TA will have the bandwidth to monitor 1-3 students this way, so pick those student samples carefully.
  • Have TAs monitor chat so you can focus on the code, and then trust the TAs to flag up concerns to you.
  • Have TAs facilitate things like breakout rooms and supporting documents, so that you don’t have to.

Find ways for 100% of students to engage.

Passively listening is the least effective way to master new material, so encourage active engagement by finding ways to offer something that you can observe or confirm 100% of students have done.

Breakout rooms:

  • Any time the curriculum says “have students do ______ or show them how to do _____” is an implicit opportunity to have students work in pairs to accomplish these goals. Whether or not it’s worth the trade-off is dependent on how much inertia goes into creating those rooms, but almost all powerful remote instruction we’ve seen so far has leveraged this capacity to have students work in small groups.
  • Have one student sharing their screen at all times in a breakout room. Students are only responsible for getting the code to work on the one shared screen.
  • When observing a breakout room, pleasantly insist that one student share their screen. They do not need to share edit access - a shared screen is all you need.
  • Resist the temptation to put students in larger groups of three, four, or more. Without defined roles, these breakout rooms are not conducive to group work larger than pairs.
  • Find some way for ALL students to know who has asked for help, and where you are currently. Whether this is a Google doc, a Slack channel, an excel spreadsheet, a kanban board, or something else doesn’t matter; but what does matter is that students know that if they ask for help, they aren’t being ignored. It’s nice to know that you’re third in line for help from a TA instead of just feeling stuck forever.

Questioning Tactics:

  • Do not ask a question without first defining how students ought to respond.
  • Students can answer open-ended questions in chat.
  • Students can answer questions with a clear right answer in a DM to you personally.
  • Students can answer larger predict-run-justify questions in breakout rooms, or in side-convo chats to one another.
  • Remember that in general, remote teaching is less directly gratifying when it comes to “reading the room” - so even though you may be tempted to ask questions that start with “who can tell me…” or “who remembers…”, you’ll find that the best case scenario here is that you’re elevating one student voice above the rest, giving yourself false confidence about how well all students have internalized the lesson. Instead, rely on questioning methods that allow 100% of students to produce a response, and then given the extra time that will take, narrow down the number of questions you’re going to ask to only the highest leverage questions.

Have a plan for stragglers.

No matter how many checkpoints you include, any group larger than about 20 students will have some portion of stragglers who were unable to follow along during the lesson - some students will consistently fall behind, and others will just have off days that you need to be ready for. Make sure you anticipate this.

On-the-spot solutions:

  • Send individual students who are stuck to a side channel or breakout room with a TA or instructor who can catch them up.
  • When you cut students loose to independent practice or small groups, have them indicate their readiness to move ahead (perhaps by having them attempt to pseudocode out their solution). Anyone who indicates that they aren’t ready (or who struggles to write their pseudocode) should be invited to stay in the main room and ask their questions in a smaller-group setting, and may even ask you to reteach the lesson in this smaller group setting.
  • Make a plan to meet 1:1 with any student who is expressing some general helplessness or frustration about the course overall.

Cultural norms (mindset norms):

  • Offer praise and thanks for any student brave enough to put something like “slow down!” or “I’m getting an error” in chat - this sort of normalization of struggle and confusion is CRITICAL for students to be able to fail without feeling like failures.
  • Offer praise for any student who is willing to ask for help from another student, and offer praise to any student will to give that help.
  • Create a system for shout-outs, and be sure to share the shout outs which specifically contribute to these cultural aims.

Set the tone with your presence.

Remote classes can feel robotic and empty, especially if you’re used to feeding off the energy of other people in the room. In a virtual classroom, their energy will not be palpable to the instructor, and so the instructor must take ownership of the room, bringing 110% of their own joy and enthusiasm to the space.

Presence tips:

  • Smile joyfully!
  • Thank every student and every TA for their response any time they contribute to the space in any way whatsover.
  • Dignify frustration with active listening, and then offer encouragement before attempting to debug.
  • Use student names (and pronounce them correctly) whenever possible.
  • Celebrate your own coding victories - give yourself high fives, ask students to tell you your hair looks great in chat, and generally find ways to model the joy you’re hoping students feel.
  • Model patience with yourself. When you make mistakes, celebrate those mistakes, own them confidently and proudly, naming what you learned after solving your own bugs, and then be really honest with students about how you expect them to handle their own mistakes with a similar unflappable confidence.

Snippets

Every day, students will provide feedback about how that day’s content went. plan to use this data as the anchor for all your reflection around the day’s lesson and how it went, and share directly with students what adjustments you’re making to day n’s lesson based on day n-1’s lesson feedback.

The more grateful and responsive you can openly be for students’ responses on feedback from the previous day, the more honest and helpful students will be on that day’s snippets.
