Mini-Project 2: Travel Page

Objectives

  • Students will ideate and work in collaborative groups.
  • Students will synthesize the content they learned in days 1-9 of this course.

What We’re Building

Students should work together to build a travel page to integrate the ideas covered so far in the course - it is advisable to follow the protocols set forth in the Project Mode guide with respect to ideation, but skip the more drawn out group formation pieces. Simply assign groups (either randomly, or according to the simpler grouping strategy of your choice) and send students on their way. Make sure to demo the below project exemplars and talk about what makes them a good project for the day.

Exemplar A - City Destination Dice

Feeling lucky? Visit David’s “City Destination Dice” travel page and get a random recommendation to visit one of the many popular tourist destinations in the world.

David’s travel page uses HTML, CSS, and JS. In the JS, there is a list of dictionaries to store the several travel destinations, querySelectors to manipulate the HTML code, and an es6+ inline arrow function that randomly selects a destination in the list.

Exemplar B - Tokyo Trip Maker

Do you dream of going to Tokyo? Find the best places to visit based on your personal interests and budget. Jeff’s Tokyo travel page allows you to dream by personalizing your experience before your trip.

Jeff uses HTML, CSS, and a couple of techniques in JS: querySelector, eventListener (input), functions, conditionals (if and else if) statements, and classList.remove('hidden') to display results.

Exemplar C - Geremy’s Library

Take a trip out to Geremy’s library! Before you go, browse through book recommendations in various genres and add them to your cart. The library will reserve them for the next time you visit.

Geremy uses HTML with Bulma and two JS functions to add books to the cart. Geremy uses querySelector with innerHTML and a conditional if statement with an array to upkeep the cart for the user.

The travel page can take any form you like, and may have any of the following functionalities:

  • Advises a user where to go based on their needs or interests
  • Helps a user decide which attractions to visit within a city or near a destination
  • Supports with travel logistics, like what to pack
  • Helps a user orient themselves to the cultural norms of a destination (e.g. reminders of whether / when bikes have the right of way, or a quiz that asks which behaviors would be frowned upon by locals)
  • Any other behavior that receives input from a user and then provides an output / outputs based on those inputs

Notes on Friday

Fridays, especially the first and second Fridays of the course, are intentionally designed with no new content. These “flex days” are critical for student success - the extra time spent without the addition of new material allows them to deepen their understanding by shifting from cognitive to autonomous performance.

This mini-project sprint that allows them to brainstorm ideas and then try to implement them is one of the best ways to offer a comprehensive review of the topics covered earlier in the week.

That said, if it has become apparent that a specific concept proved more of a stumbling block than was anticipated, that concept is critical to know moving forward, and a large subset of students are experiencing difficulty with that topic, then if an instructor wants to offer a review session for that subset of students which covers these topics in a little more detail and formality, the team should try to adjust to make that happen.
